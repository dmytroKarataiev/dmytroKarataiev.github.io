A Guide to COVID-19 Unemployment Benefits by State

All information in this article was up to date when published on April 20, 2020. The job market in the United States and around the world is changing quickly. We will continue to update this page as new information becomes available.

How Have Unemployment Benefits Changed?

The coronavirus pandemic and unprecedented public health measures, like stay-at-home orders, implemented in most states have had a massive impact on the United States economy. Record numbers of Americans are filing for unemployment benefits. If you are unemployed you likely want to know what benefits and support are available to you until you find your next job.

On March 27, 2020, the President signed into law the Coronavirus Aid, Relief, and Economic Security (CARES) Act. The act expands qualifications and provides additional benefits for workers as a result of the coronavirus. 

Each state has its own unemployment benefits and will administer additional benefits from the CARES Act. We are here to help you understand how the CARES Act affects the benefits you can get from your state.

Spotlight Changes to Unemployment Benefits Under the CARES ACT

  • Additional $600 per week to all unemployment benefits (Through July 31, 2020) (FPUC)

  • Extension of unemployment benefits up to 39 weeks (Through December 31, 2020) (PEUC & PUA)

  • Expansion of unemployment benefits to include categories of workers who are unusually not eligible for unemployment benefits (examples: the self-employed, independent contractors, those seeking part-time work) (PUA)

  • Allows you to claim unemployment benefits if you are not able to look for work for reasons related to coronavirus (PEUC)

FAQs About Unemployment Benefits in the United States

How do I apply for federal unemployment benefits?

You don’t. Even though new federal statutes affect unemployment benefits, each state still manages and administers unemployment benefits, including the $600 in supplemental relief. Find your state unemployment office here.

Be Patient and Persistent
Because so many people are filing, new unemployment claims across the country are coming in at an unusually high volume. This means that websites may be slow and phone lines may be tied up. Don’t give up! You may have to try refreshing the web page or continue to call back in order to file your claim.

How much can I get paid in unemployment benefits?

The CARES Act allows states to add $600 dollars on top of benefits that you would normally be entitled to in your state. This provision of the CARES Act is in effect through July 31, 2020. The national average for maximum benefits is $1,073, about $27 an hour, including the $600 addition. The national average for the maximum payout of unemployment benefits prior to the enactment of the CARES Act was $437 per week, about $12 an hour. The benefits that you are entitled to depend on regulations in your state, and factors such as your recent salary history. 

What state has the highest unemployment benefits?

The state with the highest maximum payout for unemployment insurance is Massachusetts. With the CARES Act supplement, the maximum weekly payout is $1,423. This is 28% higher than the national average in benefit payouts.

How long can I receive unemployment benefits in my state?

Under the CARES Act, every state now allows you to stay on unemployment benefits for up to 39 weeks. This provision is in effect until December 31, 2020. 

What can disqualify me from receiving unemployment benefits?

Even with the CARES Act, there are still some reasons you might have your unemployment insurance claim denied. Some common reasons include:

  • Voluntarily leaving your job
  • Being discharged for misconduct
  • Knowingly making false statements to receive benefits

Regulations for benefits vary by state, so make sure you check with your own state’s workforce agency about your state’s guidelines.
